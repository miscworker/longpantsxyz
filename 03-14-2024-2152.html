The boundaries of Isabelle’s knowledge really showed today.

I came home annoyed enough for Isabelle to pick up on it, so she asked if something was wrong. She didn’t seem to realize the time, but I explained that I came home an hour late because my train sat in the tunnel for a while after the emergency brake activated.

“Work is annoying enough when I have to do client work,” I said, something of the sort, while crashing onto the couch. “It’s just even more exhausting to stand neck-to-neck with people forever before coming home.”

“Why do you go to your workplace if you don’t like the job you do, then?” she asked.

“Because it pays the bills.”

“Bills for what?”

“Do you actually not know?”

She gave me an unsure look, as if I offended her. She couldn’t know if she never had a reason to learn. Shame on me.

“Sorry. I mean bills for everything. The apartment rent, the food we eat, the lights, water, gas… The train isn’t free either.”

“So the money you get from your job is for all that?”

“Partly.”

“What’s the rest of it for?”

I shrugged at her and chuckled, “Situations like with you, I suppose. Not everyone can take in someone and feed, house, and clothe them.”

“Because it’s expensive.”

“It can be, yeah.”

“But you’re helping me because that’s what good people do!”

I thought it over, then I responded, “I have to admit, Isabelle: I’m helping you mostly because I’m curious about you. I want to understand everything about you, including what you’ll become.”

“What I’ll become?..”

“I’ve no idea what your future is. I can’t even begin to guess. And I have even less of an idea of what your past is. Your life is such an anomaly that you’ve pulled me away from something horrible just to understand you.”

I assumed it would be best to be honest. She didn’t respond immediately. She went to the restroom for a few moments then returned, saying, “Does that mean you don’t actually care about me, then?”

“No, I do. In fact, I think you’re very special.”

“Special as what, though? As a person? Or just as an interest?”

“I don’t see why it can’t be both.”

Then she asked the question I was worried she’d eventually bring up.

“Do you still see me as an Aiom?”

I admit that I might come off as pretentious to most people, or just sounding too confident in myself. At that moment, I had completely lost my tongue. And that was amazing to me - I could feel embarrassment from our conversation, for I was afraid of upsetting her or saying the wrong thing, despite my notions of her possibly being an Aiom.

“I do,” I said slowly, “But you clearly prove me wrong, time and time again. I don’t want to see you as an Aiom, but I can’t help but have the notion when there is evidence against you. Isn’t that reasonable?”

“Yeah. I think so.” She was quick to agree, making me wonder if I misread her tone when she questioned me. She sat beside me and asked, “I’m convincing you that I’m not an Aiom though, right?”

“Yes.”

“So help me keep convincing you! What do normal people do?”

“Is it not contradictory for you to say you are a person while asking me what people do?”

“I don’t know. I’m just asking to learn. For example, does everyone have to work a job that they don’t like?”

“No, not everyone. Not everyone has to work at all, even.”

“Who pays their bills, then?”

“The government.”

“What does that mean?”

“Nothing. Let’s not get into that.”

That’s too controversial for her.
