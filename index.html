Oh, I dread this coming weekend. Today’s events have reminded me of the terrifying life that is idling.

After work at just before five in the evening, I received a text from an old friend, Thomas. We went to college together in California - he studied finance and advanced economics. Something of that sort, I think. For whatever reason, after we graduated, we never stayed in touch and haven’t spoken since. I recall him saying that he was heading to San Francisco to work in one of the big companies out there.

Apparently he isn’t in SF. I called him like he texted me to, and he told me he was living down in Tribeca as of last month. He invited me to come down and see him right then and there. I didn’t really have an excuse nor reason not to see him, but I didn’t think it would be a good idea.

I know for a fact that however Thomas envisioned me would be better than whatever I was now. Even if he expected me to be the same Stanley from when we both graduated, he would still be imagining a better version of myself.

Still, in my boring, unsocial life, it felt contradictory to my own needs to say no to him. So I went.

Thomas lives in a luxury condo in a newer building, one that was sandwiched right between two older ones. It was quite ugly, reminiscent of that irregularly shaped one also in Tribeca which I can’t be bothered to look up the address of. His place itself was remarkable, like the sort of apartment influencers love to show off around here (While simultaneously commenting on the city’s decline. Money through negativity).

Thomas himself is… generic. I hate to say that, but that is the most honest way I can put it. He’s become generic. Of course, I don’t recall what made him exactly unique in college, either. It’s often okay to be generic though, as long as you are a good person and friend. He talked like a bro from California while I was there, reminding me of how I used to behave around him. He’s grown out his beard and has a more adult haircut now, versus his younger clean face and raggy brown hair. The most jarring thing about him now is how muscular he’s become. It feels like we’ve switched places - I’m the scrawny looking guy now, which looks even worse given how tall I am.

His time in San Francisco was a bumpy time, he said. He got offered a job in an investing agency based in the WTC and came right over without a second thought. Good for him. He was all over me working at Aiom Corp, knowing that was my dream and all. I didn’t have the heart to tell him how I felt about it now, especially after he showed me the Aiom he had in his house. If she was just a regular servant, I would have let it go - the man had a lot of money to spare, after all - but no, she had to be a voluptuous model dressed in a typical housemaid outfit. He spoke of her objectively while she was within earshot.

Yes, that too could have been forgivable, but the way he insisted that we went out and had fun together at a bar or club just made me feel more distant from him. I know I’m absolutely wrong for thinking less of him, but I couldn’t find any maturity in him, even after hearing about his success in investing. Hell, I was there in his condo in Tribeca. It’s hard for the average man to become more successful than that.

Maybe I’m just jealous that he can have fun with ideas like that while I just feel isolated.

I tried my best to not lay any negativity onto him. I passed on his suggestion to go out, which turned into a plea before he finally accepted that I was uninterested. He said I was the ‘same ‘ole stickler who played everything straight.’ I suppose that means he didn’t detect how upset I really was.

What bothered me most about the visit though was how he actually found out I was living here. He didn’t have my number, I changed it years ago. I keep myself removed from the Internet. I suppose I would have believed him if he said he simply looked up my name in public records around here. I’m yet to meet another Longpants.

But instead, he said he reached out to my brother, just through the phone. I hadn’t talked to Steve in years, not since college. I didn’t take him to be well enough in the head to be comfortable with giving anyone my contact information. It all felt intrusive, even if nothing about it was offensive or even wrong.

No matter what way I looked at it, it disturbed me. Even now, I’m struggling to process the encounter, as mundane as it was. When I’m stuck in this sort of mindset, I can go through all sorts of reasons and answers and never come to a satisfying conclusion.

Am I scared of meeting an old friend?

Is it because they might be disappointed in what I’ve become?

Or is it, more specifically, that my idealization of our relationship was us assuming we both went on and succeeded without ever having to see the flaws which came of us inevitably? I can’t deny that I wanted to think that Thomas abstained from using Aioms, and that he wouldn’t see the ghost that I’ve become.

I don’t know.

But now I have an entire weekend of no plans to ponder over this and figure out why it bothers me so much.

An unbearable way to spend my time idling.
