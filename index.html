Ironically, after all the effort I put into setting this up, I’m not sure where to begin with these ‘ideas’. It was enough work just putting together this website. I wasn’t interested in using a template from yet another redundant, self-described ‘minimal’ site creator that is anything but minimal, so I made my own attempt in creating something from scratch.

(Of all the years I spent learning programming, HTML and whatever else are the last areas of coding that I’ve looked into)

There is a subject to bring forth: minimalism. What a controversial thing, that one.

An exhausting feature of the 21st century is minimalism, mainly in architecture and interior design. It is a prime example of the sort of problems first world citizens conjure out of thin air: in a world that is too chaotic and uneasy on the eyes, we strive to create homes, as well as workplaces, where we can relax and escape from all the details we never asked for. Details such as advertising, cluttered grocery aisles, car-infested streets; trash in the gutters, a constant phone light in one’s peripheral vision, blinding headlights despite most streets being lit well enough. We’ve created a headache for ourselves, and so we attempt to simplify our own dwellings into patches of white, gray, and black.

Places like New England and the rest of the Northeastern U.S. used to seem so romantic to me, a copy-paste suburbanite born in a cookie cutter town with no ambition in identity whatsoever. The display of wealth, no matter how small, was obvious in buildings and the community, a living proof of hardship and struggle for visitors to witness. New York City impressed me most of all with its density… I’m not interested in losing myself over a place which has made an obvious enough impression though. We all know that New York City was great.

Now it is in danger of the mundane.

Manhattan has experienced the worst of it for decades, losing its historical buildings to developers who construct soulless, overpriced condominiums in its place. The storefronts of these buildings, if they have any at all instead of a lobby assisted by a doorman, are overpriced and taken up and abandoned by one business after another, until it is taken up by a corporation like yet another fast food chain, one that will inevitably do well. And that is what real estate development in Manhattan is like: it caters only to what will inevitably succeed. The bar is too high for anyone to make an attempt unless they wish to lose everything. The only people who may afford a comfortable place to live must be willing to put all of their income towards the rent, all just for the convenience that is a major city and the stupid confidence that comes with saying, ‘I live in Manhattan.’

This would almost be fine if each of these developments were remotely interesting, but they are from it. In place of interesting masonry and details holding centuries’ worth of inspiration are flat, colorless facades learnt from only just over a half-century of modernism. The concept of beauty has been mistaken for oversimplification. Facades of the greatest buildings in the city are immaculate permanent displays of wealth - what is to be taken from a building that states that it is expensive simply due to location or its services? They display no permanence in its existence - they are current, destined to one day be swept away with the sea of change.

The city has also overbuilt itself in sight of overpopulation.

New York City, as well as any major city that is too unique in its own right, suffers from a destructive positive feedback loop. Its own popularity brings in more people than it can handle because it is unique - there are practically only two other options in all of America for settling in if you are searching for a walkable major city with great transit and reliable professional work, or at least three in total which are comparable. These three would of course be San Francisco, Chicago, and New York (Los Angeles has a long way to go). There are a great number of folks who needn’t live in New York but do so for immigration and opportunity reasons. Chicago faces harsher winters and is more off path; San Francisco has reasons I admit to being ignorant of.

New York’s prime location and prominence thus makes it too limited of a resource for too many people after it. I should know - I am an example of one of these people. My work and education did not bring me here, but rather its allure. Perhaps if the rest of the country was not paved and developed in favor of single-family suburban housing and car-centric cults, there would be more options. Of course, this is not a wonder - this is a fact. There would be limitless options in a country as enormous and populated as America.

This means that, in a place with always someone willing to rent a place at any location within Queens, Brooklyn, or Manhattan (the Bronx’s stigma is both unfair yet often true, and Staten Island is Staten Island), developers are free to make whatever atrocities they desire so long as the property returns profit in some form. An ugly apartment building is still an apartment building. It is an inevitability, I admit, but there should be enough eyes on this by now that communities begin to care, but communities too few and far in between these days.

I understand that people believe that any topic of housing is a difficult issue to address, and anything relating to the appearance of something is subjective. I disagree. Oversimplification of architecture is an eyesore, and it bleeds with consequences and is coextensive with many other matters, including the encroaching sameness of a once visually diverse city in pursuit of a greenwashed world that ignores the fundamentals of intelligent street design, all happening too fast (Just look at the Bronx and the encroaching sameness that is its sea of big box apartments).

And to think that, in sight of a still overpopulated city - in the very same city where the plague of project slums was conjured in enormous numbers - we bring up suggestions of copy-paste highrises to solve our solution of a housing shortage, all while prices continue to increase and more and more people look at the center of the world that is New York with interest.

Maybe it is not insane, but it definitely is something.
