My conversation with Isabelle yesterday had me wondering how on Earth I would teach a person how to be a person. It essentially means showing them everything that I know, or pointing them to those who I can trust.

I’m not confident in my ability to do either. I’ve felt impersonal to the world for years now - anything I can teach her might just turn her into the same pessimist that I am. But I’ve detached myself from society so much that I’ve no idea about any social circles to direct her to either.

Thank goodness she happened to run into that woman Elizabeth at the library, because I wouldn’t know who else to turn to. I may just not talk to many folks anymore, but she’s the only sensible person in recent memory I can think of that would serve as a good role model for Isabelle. She wanted to find volunteer work to do anyway, so it’s probably the morally correct path to enlightenment as a person.

I called Elizabeth and asked for her assistance in teaching Isabelle, a question which she thought to be amusing until I explained the serious nature of it. She assured me that she’s had enough kids to know how to look after someone.

“It’s adorable how much you care about her,” she told me.

Isabelle spent most of her day in Norwood doing whatever work was offered to her. She helped out at a food pantry, park cleanup… Apparently she led a class of elementary kids with a few other chaperones as they went from their school to a park for a field trip.

She also learned how to make empanadas from Elizabeth, which is now her favorite food.

“What did you take away from today’s experiences as a person, then?” I asked her.

“People are messy,” she answered, “There’s a lot of trash everywhere. Elizabeth told me that a dirty place is a reflection of the people around.”

“I wouldn’t go around saying that. It only takes a few people to dirty up a street.”

“Why’s that allowed to happen, then?”

“It’s not that it is allowed to happen. It’s more that it’s difficult to control, based on the area. There’s a lot of people in this city. You can’t track every piece of litter that they make. A lot of trash just blows in from other communities, too.”

“Ohh. You must know a lot about trash.”

“That’s not at all the case,” I laughed.

Elizabeth told me privately that she disclosed Isabelle as her Aiom to others for safety purposes, for the sake of keeping things simple. Thankfully there is an assumption (A righteous one too, I will admit) that all Aioms under legally programmed control are safe around anybody, making them an ideal choice for caregiving and children supervision.

As Elizabeth described, Isabelle was great with the children, interacting with them at their level and asking them all sorts of questions like she does with anyone else. I wonder if her behavior with children was based on her prior default Aiom AI permanently affecting her mannerisms.

“Isabelle, have you ever actually interacted with children until today?” I asked her.

“Not that I remember. Why?”

Her response pushes that idea, but I wasn’t there to witness it myself, so I can’t be sure. Personally I think Aioms being around children is really odd, given their nature. Obviously any Aiom that is intended to provide care or be around children very often isn’t going to be as scandalous as someone’s private mistress, but it’s hard to shake the stigma.

I was I think thirteen when the first Aioms were showcased. They only had two models, a male and a female. Their generically perfect design has to be intentional, in that they were both caucasian and blonde with as moderate of proportions and as healthy as one can be. It reminded me of Eye of the Beholder, an episode of The Twilight Zone. We’d become the pig people, in comparison to their perfection.

I didn’t care much for their appearance, but the fact that their behavior was split between the human brain and their AI programming was the coolest thing ever to me. In a way, it still is, although my fascination with it has turned into more of a concern of a problem which I am obligated to tend to, since nobody else seems to have the same worries I have.

Maybe everyone else - the Aioms, their consumers, their producers, advocates, anyone on their side - are the pig people, and I’m the normal person wrapped in bandages.

Probably not. I’m willing to admit that I’m an outlier, but that doesn’t make me any less wrong.

Funnily enough, if my dad was harsher on me than he already was about my interest in Aioms and was actually able to convince me to stay away from them, my life would be entirely different. It actually might be better.

But that would mean there'd be something right in his actions by discouraging me from doing what I want. It could decide the fate of the whole world and I wouldn't want that. He wouldn't have deserved that gratification in the slightest.
