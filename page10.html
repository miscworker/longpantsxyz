The downfall of architecture in the modern city has always been a stinker to me. Only a few streets in Queens alone sustain their historical elegance. At least, if the architecture alone is considered, otherwise the streets themselves are littered with the century old problem of parked cars and the new issue of greenwashed sameness.

I will advocate for anything that is anti-car as much as the next person: public transit, cycling, and whatever else. But does that mean that every single street must be turned into a road dedicated to safe traveling? When I step outside of my brownstone in Astoria, a place I now have to pay a premium for because a good amount of us folks are aware of the value of preserved neighborhoods, I’m faced with the sameness which has infected all redeveloped streets: a line of trees on either side of the street, a bike lane painted in green, and parked cars shoved in, giving just barely enough leeway for drivers to pass through. Prior to the redesign, a few trees dotted the street while cars lined the curbs bumper to bumper. All of this redesign, and the street was just as cramped as it was before… I am willing to concede to the need to support a dense neighborhood and its commuters, but at some point the street was unorganized and free for all people to pass through and it was never cramped. The automobile is the culprit, so remove it.

I admit that my personal gripe with cars probably gives me a bias towards a lot of aspects of city design. They are an exhausting subject to me - they’re overly abundant in America. Where I grew up in Suntown, California, the suburban culture was built up from scratch in the fifties for automobile owners only. Thus my childhood was decided on where my parents would choose to drive me, and not where I would choose to go. Between school and home, I had practically no other third place to spend my life. The nearest establishment to us besides another house was a dry cleaners. The road asphalt in the neighborhood baked in the sun seven months out of the year, and there wasn’t a single tree along the sidewalk for shade. There also happened to be no other children in the local neighborhood that I knew of. My friends lived about two miles away elsewhere, but walking there was not only hellish but disallowed by my parents.

And yet my father had the nerve to complain about my brother and I never going outside to do anything.

It’s no wonder that I fell into programming as an interest. Maybe even if I grew up in a fun, diverse place I still would have found an obsession with Aioms. If anything I should have been dissuaded from ever interacting with them due to my father’s influence, but perhaps it is exactly his distaste for them that drew me to them more and more, as if I unconsciously wished to defy him.

I didn’t expect to talk more about a comparably boring topic like architecture or street design for the start of this blog, considering how much of my life is actually just consumed by Aioms instead. My mind wanders more than I’d like. If it could sit still, I’d be writing a book instead, one that was perfectly paced and formatted and would be able to convince the world of its dark path. But instead, I decree this site as a dump of my thoughts and feelings.

Not to mention, it would be interesting if there was anyone out there willing to share their thoughts with me, especially if they agree.

Will America continue to promote a life of unrestrained indulgence and simplified sameness? Or is there hope? Please convince me if there is.
