In light of my aggressive views on Aioms, I imagine that there are, whether or not they contact me to say so, people who are upset by my negative portrayal of them.

I should clarify that my criticisms or opinions aren’t necessarily directed at people who own Aioms, at least not towards the average consumer. It is directed at a capitalistic economy that prioritizes profits and artificial happiness before the health and well-being of humans. It is humanity at fault, not the person - and yes, I mean humanity, not only the few with the power to influence the world.

Those who are upset, including those who I have upsetted in the past, usually criticize me for viewing the use of Aioms in a largely sexual lens. Rationalize your ownership of an Aiom however you want, but there is something undeniably perverted in purchasing a lifeform bred for one’s pleasure, whether sexual or not. We have come a long way to denounce slavery along with all forms of discrimination, at least here in America, and yet we are embracing the commodity of life all over again because we suffer from mass amnesia. The phrase ‘Those who ignore history are doomed to repeat it’ is true because the phrase itself is time-tested across humanity’s existence.

Mass amnesia is a long topic that merits its own post here, but, for the fairness of my critics, let’s talk about Aioms while ignoring the perverted nature of them or the fact that we have built a slave industry.

Aioms are advertised, first and foremost, as personal companions. I am aware of how this is life changing for many folks; it mirrors the controversies surrounding the increasing use in AI chatbots and digital partners from several years ago. It is easy to call anyone who uses those social supplements a pathetic failure, but there are folks who are doomed to never find a natural way to satisfy themselves in the real world. The handicapped and mentally challenged are at the forefront of these folks. It is not fair to expect someone with a muscle-eating disease or severe social problems to be able to find a partner as easily as the next person. It is not even fair to expect any normal person to always be able to find someone so easily. It was never easy for even myself, and to this day it still is not.

Truthfully, some people are also just pathetic failures due to a million coincidences, doomed to never find love or even good friends. But this is not just a symptom exclusive to them - this is a symptom derived from the environment we’ve created for ourselves.

The environment for seeking connections has evolved drastically since the beginning of the 20th century. In the 20th century, troubled romantics took out small section ads in the newspaper crying for a lover of desirable type and look. People could respond by sending a letter or even meeting them at a particular location at a set time listed in the ad. In the 1980s, video dating emerged, a format where subscribers recorded video tapes of themselves to put together a profile, sent their information and recording to a company, then the company distributed these profiles to other subscribers deemed a potential match.

The 1990s and onward established internet dating, an era most of us are probably familiar with, although the presentation of dating sites and chat rooms has been massively different in each decade since then. The ability to communicate instantaneously with a potential match through email or otherwise outclassed all other forms of dating. So much so that this has become the cultural norm for most of America.

The similarities in each of these formats is that it implies that the people using them have failed socially in their current environment. Otherwise, why would they resort to using such an inauthentic way of meeting new people?

This is reasonable to see occur in small amounts. Eccentric people exist, as well as physically or mentally challenged people. Again, even people exhibiting normal behavior and physical traits can just so happen to suffer from the challenge that is making friends, let alone finding lovers.

So what does it mean when the average American socially fails in their environment?

It means that our environment is consistently acting against our social needs as humans.

The sameness which has encroached across New York City and its streets and architecture has been several decades in the making in the rest of the United States and Canada. All one has to do to verify this is to look at what a majority of American media depicts: roads, cars, and suburban cities. The average American lives over a mile away to the nearest grocery story. The average American commutes to work by car. The average American is burdened with car payments, increasing fuel prices, high insurance costs, inevitable maintenance fees, and the constant danger of sharing the road with maniacs somehow licensed to drive two ton vehicles.

(Of course, a lot of American media also depicts our major cities and their massively different lifestyles, but these are often less associated with being ‘familiar social fulfillments’ that say a typical sitcom of a suburban family slots into. Instead they are often associated with either the outlandish nature or events that come from living in a major city - New York, L.A., and the Bay Area come to mind - or how alien or crime-ridden these places are. I am willing to concede from this argument as being biased towards what I have been exposed to, but there is still truth in certain elements being overexposed compared to others in media. Perhaps not a fair point regardless.)

I can dance around the exhausting facts of automobile centric society all day, but the cataclysmic effect of such a society that (also) goes ignored is the social isolation we have all accepted as being normal. The requirements for simply being around others besides our immediate household, school peers, or work peers is so ridiculous when compared to the traditional development pattern of long ago that we invest most of our life with inauthentic means of fulfilling our social needs.

I remember visiting my grandmother who eventually settled in Suntown with us, in her own home a bit of ways across town. Due to her blindness, she was unable to drive, so she either had to have one of my parents come to take her somewhere, which would require driving all the way to her, or to call for a cab. The inconvenience grew obvious and she felt ashamed in asking for a lift so often, so she eventually stopped and became a homebody.

Besides, there was little for her to do anyway, compared to her last home in a small town in Florida. There, she had various third places to go to: an informal park by the creek, an active local diner, a classic drugstore that still had a bar for shakes, a library, an outdoor theater, a spot to throw horseshoes, an active church (She was not religious or of any faith, but she enjoyed the community presence there). All of these places in her town were privately owned by local residents. I believe the only franchised or chain location was a gas station at the edge of town.

Instead of spending the last eight years of her life among an active community of residents familiar with one another, she spent it watching detective shows and Hallmark movies on repeat while playing 3-match games on her tablet. The closest thing she had to a community was Facebook, where, based on every experience I had on there, was only used for either sharing memes, or complaining about politics or not being able to park somewhere in town (Elderly folks with nothing better to do aren’t exactly the most positive).

Some folks might think that the issue was simply that she could not drive, but that just avoids the problem of the environment itself which we develop. Suburban sprawl provides no culture or chances for authentic communities to come together naturally or consistently. Even financially it is a burden on the average American, barring many people from opening businesses in places where it is completely unsustainable due to low density, allowing only chain stores and restaurants to set up in enormous big box stores only to inevitably abandon them after at least a decade later (Which then leaves an oversized building useless to every establishment that does not use these big box stores, still barring private business owners).

Now imagine this cultural death affecting our youth, yearning for love and excitement. The only practical and accessible means for young Americans would be social media, including dating apps, a modern obsession. It comes as no surprise that a boring environment produces teenage mischief and uninspired people who grow out of their ambitions and fall into unproductive lifestyles, such as normalized day-to-day gaming, pornography addiction, vaping and smoking (or anything with nicotine or THC), unhealthy eating habits. All of which contributes to a sedentary lifestyle.

It should be noted that, purely in the context of North America culture, suburbs are not the prime cause of these issues. It is simply any environment where community is discouraged due to the infrastructure involved, the sameness which has encroached upon the area, or because of external issues such as a failing economy or major events which causes a shift in behavior (In a healthy community or environment, residents should be able to properly react and rectify the consequences of these events).

Sameness and discouraged engagement in a community will always diminish the full social potential of a person. Only the most eccentric or socially oblivious will be able to shine in these habitats, and often they are pushed away or are forced to find their own people, further separating the leaders from the meek. Such effects bring on a failing community of unambitious folks incapable of adapting to change and those whose practices will bring down the community further.

… In all this, I may have lost sight of my point and not realized how much I rambled here. What all this points to, though, is that Aioms are yet another example of solving a human-made problem of lack of social fulfillment. One does not have to be prude or hateful of humanity’s sexual desires to acknowledge this. In fact, embracing human love and sex should be coextensive with denouncing the use of Aioms and all related fulfilling technologies and practices.

For, when we make a world where it is impossible to be authentically happy, we can only achieve inauthentic happiness through artificial means.
