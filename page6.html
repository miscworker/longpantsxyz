Before I talk about Isabelle, I want to comment on something I saw today. I’m going to omit the product name because I don’t care to advertise for them (Explaining the ad is extensive enough.)

There are these ads for a social media app in the subway stations, split as a two part ad on two separate posters. The first is a blank white canvas with typeface font text reading nothing but ‘Less social media.’ It features zero other labels.

The other is a poster with an obnoxious amount of artificial content, captioned ‘More (Social media name).’ It links to a website describing the intent of their social media app versus others. It explains how social media apps make people unhappy and feel like strangers to others, an undoubtedly true fact. It then goes on to say why their app is different and how it apparently makes its users happier. One feature of its app it clarifies is that conversations made on there automatically delete because then it is more like ‘real life.’

The claims it makes about the issues of social media are true, but, as usual, it is all pushed just in the goal of selling a product. Maybe what irritates me the most is just the deliberately cluttered ad full of artificial, manufactured monstrosities that are apparently supposed to imply something personal or real? I don’t understand it.

There is nothing heartfelt ever about pushing a product onto others, especially ones engineered to be addictive for young people especially. Perhaps such things wouldn’t be necessary if the country wasn’t itself manufactured or artificial.

Isabelle cleaned the house further out of boredom. This is when I came to the obstacle that was her apparent lack of personality.

When I arrived home, she told me she had stayed inside all day, just like I requested, but she ‘needed to do something or else.’ Aioms are normally hardwired to never feel bored, so I imagine that the sensation of waiting for something to happen but being forced to stay put probably drove her mad.

Although, if she claims to not be an Aiom, there is nothing actually stopping her from going out if she pleases. I realized this and decided that her own sanity is more important than her safety, as disagreeable as most would find that, and I took her out to make a copy of my keys for her.

I only realize now, considering that she has only been in the house for about three days, how strange my decision is. At the same time, I can’t be bothered to care.

In an effort to respect her, I explained to her the realism of the situation.

“I know you say that you’re not an Aiom,” I said, something of the sort, “And I’ll believe you for as long as you say so. However, here in New York*, you will be confused for an Aiom. This means that you might be treated differently by people around here if you aren’t with me. Sometimes, if you aren’t doing certain things or acting a certain way, you’ll be asked what your ‘current errand’ is. This is because Aioms are made to serve humans, so that’s what you’d be expected to do: to serve me, wherever I might be.

“If you’re ever asked what you’re doing, and you’re unsure, just say you’re out on an errand for your owner. Otherwise, I think you should be alright.”

*I find it easier to imply that things are different here in the city, just to avoid any confusion with how the world works.

She thanked me and immediately tried to go on her way in a random direction, until I asked if she knew how to make her way home. Of course she didn’t. I suggested that she first let me show her the city before she went around on her own.

While at home as I made dinner (Pasta is all I'm capable of cooking with confidence), she brought up an interesting question to me.

“Stanley, what do you do all day?”

She expected a very precise answer. I told her I work on weekdays, eight hours a day. I spend probably an hour and a half commuting everyday, at max. I sleep between six and eight hours every night. That leaves me with six to eight and a half hours everyday. If I spend another two hours eating, waking up, and getting ready for work, then I have at least four hours every day to do whatever I please.

“What do you do in those four hours, then?” she asked.

“Not really anything.”

“You do nothing?”

“I do not do ‘really anything.’”

“What does that mean?”

It didn’t really mean anything. I don’t have much else to do. But I just told her that it means I do whatever has to be taken care of. When that answer didn’t suffice, I just told her that I’m not certain.

It was an uncomfortable question for me. If she is this sort of blank canvas of a person who knows little about anything, then posing an equally boring impression upon her cannot be exactly a great entry into the real world.

So I corrected myself to her and admitted that I’m obsessed with Aioms, and similar concepts as well. The artificial methods of technology used to fulfill human needs in place of consequently lost natural methods fascinates me.

“That’s why you work at Aiom Corp!” she said.

“Rightly so,” I said. And other reasons, I thought.

She asked about my work, and I described my job and whatever else. She said I must be really smart to do something like that. I didn’t really have much of a response - thanking her didn’t sit right with me.

“Hey, could I get a job?”

“I don’t know. Just a job.”

“What do you want to do?”

“I don’t know.”

“What are you good at, then?”

“Do you think I’m good at cleaning?”

I smirked. My reaction is probably akin to some sort of cultural or demographic concept that’s better to avoid, but I didn’t want to lead her down just doing something she would have been stuck doing as an Aiom.

“Sure, but there’s a lot of other things you could try learning. Is there anything in the world that you like?”

“I don’t know.”

“Try to actually think about it first.”

She exaggeratedly entered deep thought, sitting like the Thinker sculpture on the couch. I imagined that there had to be some lingering memories within her that could create a preference for something.

“I think,” she said, very slowly like she was unsure, “That I like to see people be happy.”

I imagine that this statement was born out of the Aiom AI influence on the brain, even being deactivated (If it was, anyway). The AI ensures Aiom well-being by guaranteeing a positive response in the brain when satisfying owners, or humans in general, in whatever way they’re asked. If I’m correct, this permanently restructures the brain. Brainwashing, essentially.

But… at least it’s vague enough to allow for many opportunities, right?

“That’s good,” I told her, “There’s a lot of ways to make people happy.”

“Like how?”

“I think you should find that out yourself.”

“How do I do that, then?”

This is generally how most of our conversations have gone so far. It is very difficult to not condescend to her by mistake.

It makes me feel even more pathetic to think that this is the most I’ve interacted with someone outside of work or therapy since college.
