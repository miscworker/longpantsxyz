Isabelle’s constant state of awe during our sight-seeing trip yesterday reminds me of the lack of such fascination I experienced in my youth.

It makes me feel a pit in my stomach when I hear people talk so fondly about their own childhood, as if it was the better part of their life. To this day I am so grateful to have escaped from that hellish era, and yet I can’t help but feel ashamed of myself for not being happier. I was housed, clothed, fed… I had as much technology as any other American. I wasn’t abused, at least I don’t think I was.

But I was so utterly bored. I’d argue that that is the worst illness any child can have. For it was through that boredom which came the disgusting beast which resided within me, made clear by my own father. He yelled at me and punished me for something I didn’t understand. What my young self took away from that was to keep my own interests to myself. All he ever did was either ridicule me or talk about himself anyway.

To this day, I still don’t entirely understand the beast. It’s me, all in the flesh and mind, and yet it feels like a different entity entirely.

I remember one night as a young teen, when I was most enslaved by my desires, I might have experienced sleep paralysis. I’m not sure what it was, but I recall floating just a hair off of my bed - enough to notice it, not enough to be certain of it - and, towards the end of my room, was some nightmarish demon, horned with a long beak and black holes for eyes. It was enormous, hunching over to contain itself in the small space of the room. Below its waist, it stroked itself with a tentacle, staring at me as it did so. I swear that I could feel it.

That memory resurfaces every once in a while. I don’t know at all what was happening, except for the fact that it wasn’t real.

Now that I think about it, Aioms are saved from all the misery that comes with having relentless desires such as what I’m burdened with. They either don’t have any desires, or their desires are controlled and limited as necessary. They have no reason to have shame because they are programmed to believe they are righteous in what they do.

It seems sinful to play god and manipulate humans like so, but is it wrong to free any living thing of undesirable experiences when possible?

It doesn’t seem like a question that anyone is ever meant to answer, at least through practice.

I wonder if Isabelle is free of the misery that comes with shame and temptation. I would say, for her own well-being, I hope so. Then again, I would hope not, because that would make her all the more human.
