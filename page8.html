I’ve heard of this apparently common scam Leah describes, where people use Aioms to pose as loved ones in exchange for a ransom from the target victim. I can’t say that this is not real, but this practice makes no sense and requires too much effort that any person willing to take so many steps for a crime would realize it’s not worth bothering.

And yet its spread as a rumor, all for the sensationalism and attention it brings on social media (And the news, but the news might as well be considered social media nowadays considering its clickbaity nature). Probably a reason the rumor succeeds is the average person’s unfamiliarity with an Aiom’s limitations, usually placing unrealistic expectations on them.

First of all, as I described from the Aiom creation process in a previous entry, Aioms are created from the start to be the same forever. Their appearance is based on their genetics, not some shapeshifting nonsense. The only reason you would be able to convince a scam victim that an Aiom is their loved one is if they just so happened to look like them, and, even then, there are dozens of protocols in place to prevent this from happening.

Again, I can’t say that this scenario is impossible, because it is inevitable that an Aiom will look like a real person, the same way there are lookalikes normally already. But the scenario just does not make sense.

Say I am the criminal, and I just so happen to have an Aiom that looks like a relative or friend of yours.

First of all, you have to be out of contact enough with that person to not be able to reach them to verify their safety. Secondly, how am I delivering this threat to you? In person? Over the internet? A phone call? If it is in person, well, that is just trying to get myself caught. And the Aiom would likely be realized if they were seen in person.

If this was done over any digital medium at all, the Aiom becomes pointless. We have countless AI tools and already skilled criminals who can use editing tools, voice modulators and synthesis, and whatever else to convince someone of a lie. Bringing an Aiom into the operation contributes nothing.

In regards to Aioms committing crimes in general though: honestly, it is more sparse than I would expect it to be. That is, sparse in the public view. I think I already made a point of Aioms being reprogrammed in illegal off-market operations. I can assume that many are altered for sexual purposes, considering how controlled the BDSM roleplay functions of their programming are. That’s a whole rabbit hole I’m not interested in diving into at this moment.

For Aioms which are made to not be trackable - I would love to meet the folks capable of dismantling the relevant devices, because they are prone to go off on the network as soon as there is deliberate interference with them in most cases. They’d have to be geniuses.

I won’t get into the specifics because I don’t want people getting any ideas, but it’s definitely not an easy task.
